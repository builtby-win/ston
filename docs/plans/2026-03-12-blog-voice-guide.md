# Blog Voice Guide

## What the voice feels like

The voice is personal, conversational, opinionated, and a little unhinged in a fun way.

It should feel like a smart builder talking directly to a friend on the internet, not a polished company blog trying to sound authoritative. The writing is strongest when it sounds lived-in: real frustrations, real numbers, real preferences, real outcomes.

There is usually a mix of:

- casual language
- strong opinions
- specific personal examples
- little jokes or side comments
- moments of sincerity underneath the chaos

The writing should sound human before it sounds impressive.

## Core voice traits

### 1. Lowercase and conversational

Default to lowercase prose unless there is a good reason not to. The tone should sound spoken, like you are thinking out loud but still making a clear point.

### 2. Real over polished

Do not smooth out the edges too much. Keep the parts that feel like a real person reacting in real time.

Good:

- "sounds simple right?"
- "absolute nightmare"
- "i'm frankly blown away"

Bad:

- "this solution presented several implementation challenges"
- "users may find this workflow beneficial"

### 3. Specificity earns the opinion

Strong opinions work best when they are backed by direct experience, examples, screenshots, numbers, or stories.

Do not say a tool is great or bad in a generic way. Say what happened, what it let you do, what broke, or what changed in your workflow.

### 4. Builder energy

The perspective is from someone actively making things, shipping, testing tools, hitting weird limitations, and changing opinions fast.

The writing should feel close to the workbench.

### 5. Emotional honesty without overexplaining

Even in technical or tooling posts, there is usually a real emotional layer: frustration, excitement, relief, gratitude, obsession, disbelief. Keep that layer, but do not turn every paragraph into therapy.

### 6. Slightly playful, never fake

Humor is good when it sounds natural. Small exaggerations, little asides, and self-aware phrasing help.

But avoid trying to be quirky every sentence. The post still needs a backbone.

## Writing rules

- lead with a strong opinion, tension, or lived reality
- use concrete examples quickly
- prefer short and medium sentences over long formal ones
- keep transitions natural, not essay-like
- let some phrases sound spoken instead of perfectly edited
- keep a few sharp one-liners when they hit
- do not sound like a content marketer
- do not sound like a detached tutorial writer
- do not use fake authority or generic productivity language

## Phrases and moves that fit

- "sounds simple, right?"
- "here's the thing"
- "the difference is staggering"
- "i was blown away"
- "wtf"
- "and it's not just that"
- "honestly? probably not"
- "it sounds insane and it is"
- "pretty satisfying when it finally worked"

Use these as references, not as a template to spam.

## Phrases and moves to avoid

- "in today's fast-paced landscape"
- "whether you're a founder, creator, or entrepreneur"
- "seamlessly"
- "leverage"
- "optimize your workflow"
- "this comprehensive guide"
- "game-changing" unless used ironically

## Structure patterns that already work

### Technical post pattern

- hook with pain, tension, or ambition
- explain what you wanted
- show what broke
- walk through the solution
- end with a real opinionated takeaway

### Opinion post pattern

- provocative opening line
- direct personal evidence
- broader take on the market or trend
- practical takeaway or prediction

### Personal post pattern

- simple statement
- personal story
- reflection
- memorable closing line

## Editing checklist

- does this sound like a person saying it out loud?
- is there at least one real detail that proves the point?
- did we keep the opinion sharp?
- did we remove corporate or generic phrasing?
- does the post sound like it came from someone who actually uses these tools every day?
- is there at least one line that feels quotable or memorable?

## Humanizer pass

When rewriting a draft, make it:

- less polished
- more direct
- more specific
- more spoken
- more emotionally honest

Do not make it sloppy. Make it feel alive.
